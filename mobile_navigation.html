Mobile Navigation Unification

Confidentiality Notice: To respect the intellectual property and non-disclosure agreements of Epic Systems, I have abstracted or omitted specific details from this case study. Please contact me if you would like to learn more about my work and process.

Overview

I led the redesign of the navigation UI for Epic's clinical mobile suite (Rover, Haiku, and Canto). Over the course of a year, I consolidated over a decade's worth of fragmented designs into a single, unified system for iOS and Android. By aligning siloed development teams and validating workflows with clinicians, I established a consistent navigation model that improved efficiency for users and reduced the training burden for healthcare organizations.

The Problem

Epic's mobile apps evolved independently to serve different clinical roles, such as nurses and physicians. As clinical workflows grew to overlap across these apps, the navigation became fragmented, leading to three core challenges:

  • Navigation Friction: Inconsistent patterns across the suite made navigation unintuitive and inefficient, often requiring excessive taps to perform routine tasks.
  • Operational Overhead: Healthcare organizations faced high maintenance costs as they had to manage disparate training materials for the same workflows across different apps and operating systems (iOS and Android).
  • Design & Development Debt: Implementing new features required separate design decisions and UI implementations for each app. This duplication of effort increased design debt and created unnecessary complexity when scaling new clinical tools.

The Goal

To design a single, scalable navigation system used across all clinical mobile apps to ensure a consistent user experience and a more streamlined development process.

User Research & Validation

I led the research and alignment process to make sure the new UI worked for clinicians and was actually buildable:

  • Shadowing: Observed and interviewed nurses and physicians in hospitals and clinics to see how they actually handle mobile devices on the job.
  • Usability Testing: Ran task-based tests at hospitals, clinics, and clinical conferences to make sure the new navigation was intuitive and efficient.
  • Expert Review: Worked with internal and external clinicians to make sure that the design didn't disrupt existing workflows.
  • Stakeholder Reviews & Iteration: Presented designs to Product, Dev, and QA stakeholders to gather feedback. I iterated on the UI based on their feedback and technical constraints.

Design

I designed a unified navigation system that works across iOS and Android and scales between phone and tablet screens. Some key improvements were:

  • Consistent Interactions: Standardized where core actions live so the experience is predictable across the entire suite.
  • Persistent Patient Identity: Ensured patient information is always visible. This allows clinicians to reference patient data at a glance without having to navigate away from their current workflow.
  • Platform Parity: Aligned Android and iOS patterns so the apps feel like part of the same family.

Outcomes

  • Team Alignment: Navigated multi-team ownership to get formal sign-off, establishing a shared roadmap for mobile development.
  • Scalable System: Created a standardized framework that serves as the foundation for new mobile features, so teams don't have to "re-invent" navigation for every new tool.
  • Simpler Training: Established a consistent UI that makes it easier for healthcare organizations to consolidate their internal training materials, reducing the long-term documentation and support burden for their IT departments.
